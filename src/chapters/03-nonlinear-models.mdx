# Non-Linear Models

Real-world data rarely follows a straight line.

Consider predicting the trajectory of a ball:

$$y = -4.9t^2 + v_0 t + h_0$$

Or diminishing returns on ad spend вҖ” doubling your budget doesn't double your sales.

## Adding Non-Linearity

We can extend our model with polynomial terms:

$$y = w_1 x + w_2 x^2 + b$$

Or with more features:

$$y = w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + w_3 x_1 x_2 + b$$

The training process is identical вҖ” gradient descent still works. We just have more parameters to tune.

<Callout type="idea">
The jump from linear to non-linear is crucial: it lets models capture curves, interactions, and complex patterns. But manually choosing the right non-linear terms (is it $x^2$? $\log x$? $\sqrt{x}$?) requires domain expertise.
</Callout>

## The Problem with Manual Feature Engineering

For house prices, maybe $x^2$ works. But what about:
- Recognizing faces in photos (millions of pixels)
- Understanding natural language (infinite combinations of words)
- Predicting protein structures

We can't manually decide which non-linear combinations matter. We need a model that can **learn its own non-linear features**.

> This is exactly what neural networks do вҖ” and why they're so powerful.

## From Hand-Crafted to Learned Features

| Approach | Non-Linearity | Effort |
|----------|--------------|--------|
| Linear regression | None | Zero |
| Polynomial regression | $x^2, x^3, \ldots$ | Manual choice |
| Feature engineering | $\log x$, interactions | Domain expertise |
| **Neural networks** | **Learned automatically** | **Just provide data** |

<Callout type="analogy">
Hand-crafting features is like writing specific rules: "if the email contains 'Nigerian prince', it's spam." Neural networks instead learn: "here are patterns I've found across millions of emails that correlate with spam." Much more general.
</Callout>
